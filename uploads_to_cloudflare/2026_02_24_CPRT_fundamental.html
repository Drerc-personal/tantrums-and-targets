COPART, INC. (NASDAQ: CPRT)

THE ARBITER'S ANALYSIS — February 24, 2026

As-of date: February 24, 2026. All prices and ratios use $37.45 unless otherwise noted. 52-week range: $33.81 – $63.85. Source: Yahoo Finance

Central question: Does this company deserve a spot on a concentrated watchlist of the best businesses in the world?

Short answer: KEEP. Best-of-breed salvage auction duopolist with a fortress balance sheet, physical moat, and secular tailwinds. Current Q2 weakness is cyclical (soft claims volumes), not structural. We own 60 shares at $34.97 via GTC fill. Hold position, monitor for ADD at $32.

SECTION 1: BUSINESS IDENTITY

Core business: Copart operates the world's largest online salvage vehicle auction platform, connecting insurance companies and other vehicle sellers with ~1 million registered buyers across 185+ countries.

How it makes money: FY2025 full-year revenue was $4.65B. Revenue mix by segment (Q2 FY2026 and H1 FY2026, as reported):

Category Q2 FY2026 H1 FY2026 Description
Service revenues (U.S.) $751.8M $1,573.3M Fees for facilitating auctions — storage, towing, listing, selling (consignment model)
Vehicle sales (U.S.) $169.8M $334.7M Vehicles Copart buys and resells (principal model)
Service revenues (Intl) $156.3M $293.5M Same as U.S., primarily UK, Germany, Brazil, Spain
Vehicle sales (Intl) $43.8M $75.7M Principal model internationally
Total $1,121.7M $2,277.2M

Source: Copart Q2 FY2026 press release (copart.com/investorrelations), also available via Business Wire

Service revenue is the profit engine (~80% of total). The consignment model means Copart never owns the vehicle — it facilitates the auction for a fee. Vehicle sales (principal model) carries inventory risk but is a smaller and shrinking portion as international operations shift from principal → consignment. Insurance companies declaring vehicles total losses supply the majority of Copart's volume. Source: Copart FY2025 10-K, Item 1 (SEC EDGAR)

Most recent quarter (Q2 FY2026, ended Jan 31, 2026): Revenue $1.12B (-3.6% YoY). Service revenue declined 4.0%. Net income $350.7M (-9.5%). EPS $0.36 vs $0.40 prior year, missing consensus of ~$0.39. U.S. insurance units declined 10.7%. However, excluding catastrophe-related vehicles, revenue grew +1.3%. ASPs rose 6% for U.S. insurance consignors. Source: Copart Q2 FY2026 press release, GuruFocus Q2 highlights

Who the customer is: Sellers are insurance companies (State Farm, Allstate, GEICO, Progressive, etc.), financial institutions, fleet operators, rental car companies, and dealers. Buyers include vehicle dismantlers, rebuilders, exporters, used vehicle dealers, and the general public. Copart states it connects consignors to "approximately 1 million members in over 185 countries" and operates "over 250 locations in 11 countries." Source: Copart Q2 FY2026 press release. No single customer is >10% of revenue. Source: Copart FY2025 10-K (SEC EDGAR)

Moat type: PHYSICAL + NETWORK. Copart and IAA (now part of RB Global) are the two dominant players in U.S. salvage vehicle auctions. The market is highly concentrated — Copart's 10-K notes competitive dynamics but does not disclose exact share figures. Source: Copart FY2025 10-K (SEC EDGAR). The physical moat is the hardest to replicate: 250+ locations requiring land acquisition, zoning, and environmental permits — with years-long lead times.

Revenue deceleration is notable. FY2023–FY2025 grew at ~10% annually. H1 FY2026 is now -1.4%, driven by Q2's -3.6% decline. The cause: U.S. insurance unit volumes declined 10.7% in Q2. Interpretation: The cyclical framing is likely correct, but there is a structural tail risk: if consumers permanently shift toward higher deductibles ($1,000+) and forgo collision coverage entirely, the pool of potential total losses shrinks even as total loss frequency within that pool rises. SECTION 4: MOAT ASSESSMENT

What protects this company from competition? - Physical infrastructure (PHYSICAL moat): 250+ locations across 11 countries. Purpose-built storage requiring land acquisition, environmental permits, zoning. Years-long lead times. CapEx of $537M in FY2025 alone. - Duopoly structure: Two dominant players control the vast majority of U.S. salvage auctions, with the next competitor holding a small fraction. Source: Copart 10-K (SEC EDGAR) - Network effects: ~1M registered buyers in 185+ countries. More buyers → higher ASPs → more sellers → more buyers. Record auction returns and ASPs (up 6% YoY in Q2) confirm this flywheel. - Technology platform: VB3 auction, Copart 360 imaging, IntelliSeller AI. Fully online since 2003. - Switching costs: Insurance companies integrate Copart into claims workflows. Switching is operationally painful with minimal upside.

Moat trend: SAME. The duopoly structure is stable. Total loss frequency continues to rise (22.6% in first 9 months of 2025, up ~1 point YoY). Source: Autobody News. Structural tailwinds (older vehicle fleet, rising repair costs, ADAS complexity, EV battery damage) remain intact.

Closest competitor: IAA (RB Global/Ritchie Bros.) IAA is improving its AuctionNow platform and buyers report smoother experience. Source: Auto4Export. However, Copart has consistently invested more heavily in land and physical capacity — $537M in CapEx in FY2025 alone. Physical capacity is the bottleneck, and Copart appears to be investing more aggressively than IAA in expanding it.

AI disruption test: LOW. AI is a tool for Copart, not a threat to Copart. CEO Liaw on Q2 call: the company has integrated AI across imaging, auction pricing, and productivity, and views its physical moats (storage capacity, global buyer base, regulatory knowledge) as the core defense. Source: GuruFocus Q2 highlights. AI cannot replace the need for physical land to store wrecked cars.

SECTION 5: RISKS & THREATS

Regulatory/legal risks: MODERATE — DOJ investigation ongoing. - Fact: The U.S. Department of Justice is investigating Copart for potential violations of money laundering laws relating to practices and procedures for preventing illicit financial activity by its members. First disclosed Q1 FY2024 (Oct 2023 letter from DOJ). The company is cooperating but states it "is unable to predict the timing, scope, or result of any potential governmental, criminal, or civil proceeding." Source: Copart 10-Q for Q2 FY2026 (SEC EDGAR) - Interpretation: This is a real risk with unpredictable outcome. Copart implemented a formal Global Anti-Money Laundering Policy (effective April 2024) in response. The $5.1B cash balance means even a large fine would not threaten solvency. But a DOJ consent decree could impose ongoing compliance costs — a potential permanent "margin tax" of 100–200 bps on an already-compressing margin base.

Competitive threats (specific, named): - Fact: IAA (RB Global) is upgrading its AuctionNow platform with improved speed, bidding experience, and title processing. Source: Auto4Export - Interpretation: IAA is a credible competitor improving its technology, but the duopoly structure has been stable for years with no meaningful share shifts. Copart's physical infrastructure investment gap (3x+ CapEx) is widening, not narrowing.

Macro/cyclical sensitivity: MODERATE — currently the primary headwind. - Fact: U.S. insurance units declined 10.7% in Q2 FY2026. Management attributed this to softer claims counts from reduced auto insurance purchasing. Source: Copart Q2 FY2026 earnings call (GuruFocus) - Fact: Potential U.S. auto tariffs could affect international buyer demand (38.8% of U.S. units go to international buyers). Source: Finimize - Structural tail risk (per Gemini): If consumers permanently shift toward higher deductibles and forgo collision coverage, the insured pool shrinks structurally. Monitor policies-in-force data from Progressive, GEICO, and Allstate as a leading indicator. If policies flatline while miles driven increases, the "cyclical" thesis weakens.

Management/insider activity: NEUTRAL (option-exercise selling under pre-scheduled plans). - Fact: CEO Jeffrey Liaw sold 25,137 shares on Jan 15, 2026 at $40.17 (~$1M), a 31% reduction in direct holdings. Executed under a pre-scheduled Rule 10b5-1 plan adopted April 2025. Director Thomas Tryforos sold 100,000 shares at $39.07 (~$3.9M) on Nov 25, 2025. Source: SEC Form 4 filings via TradingView - Interpretation: CEO compensation includes 10-year RSU lockups (to 2032) and performance-hurdled options — strong long-term alignment. Selling is option-exercise driven under pre-scheduled plans, not discretionary. No insider purchases, but the pattern is routine.

Customer concentration risk: LOW. No single customer >10% of revenue. Diversified across major insurance carriers.

SECTION 6: THESIS & EXIT CRITERIA

Original thesis: Copart is one half of a durable duopoly in online salvage vehicle auctions, protected by a physical infrastructure moat that takes years and billions to replicate, with secular tailwinds from rising total loss frequency, an aging vehicle fleet, and increasing vehicle repair complexity (ADAS, EVs).

Nature of risk: CYCLICAL. The Q2 revenue miss is volume-driven (fewer insured drivers → fewer claims), not structural (moat erosion or competitive share loss). Total loss frequency continues to rise. The business model is intact.

Exit criteria (from our system): "Insurance carriers vertically integrate salvage; total loss rates decline structurally."

Has exit criteria been triggered or approached? - Fact: Total loss frequency rose to 22.6% in first 9 months of 2025, up ~1 point YoY. Source: Autobody News - Fact: No evidence of insurance carrier vertical integration into salvage auctions. - Interpretation: Exit criteria NOT TRIGGERED and not approaching.

Thesis status: INTACT.
