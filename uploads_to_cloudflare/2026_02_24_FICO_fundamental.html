As-of date: February 24, 2026 (intraday). All prices and ratios use $1,283 unless otherwise noted. 52-week range: $1,268 – $2,218. Source: TradingView, MacroTrends

Central question: Does this company deserve a spot on a concentrated watchlist of the best businesses in the world?

Short answer: KEEP (WATCH). Still exceptional economics, but moat is narrowing and valuation is not cheap — hold small, don't add.

SECTION 1: BUSINESS IDENTITY

Core business: FICO provides predictive analytics software and the dominant consumer credit scoring model used by virtually all U.S. lenders for underwriting decisions.

How it makes money (FY2025 full year, ended Sep 30, 2025 — $1.991B total revenue):

Segment Revenue % of Total YoY Growth
Scores (B2B + B2C) ~$1.169B ~59% +27%
Software (Platform + Non-platform) ~$822M ~41% +5%

Source: FICO FY2025 Annual Report (investors.fico.com), FICO Q1 2026 Press Release

The Scores segment is the profit engine — extremely high-margin licensing revenue generated every time a lender pulls a FICO score. B2B scores (banks, mortgage lenders, card issuers) are the bulk. The Software segment sells decision management platforms, fraud detection, and analytics tools. Platform ARR is growing 33% but non-platform ARR is declining 8%.

Most recent quarter (Q1 FY2026, ended Dec 31, 2025): Revenue of $512M (+16% YoY). Scores revenue surged 29% to $304.5M, driven by a 36% increase in B2B revenue from higher mortgage origination pricing and volume. Software revenue grew 2% to $207.5M. Source: FICO Press Release via Yahoo Finance

Who the customer is: Financial institutions — banks, credit unions, mortgage lenders, credit card issuers, auto lenders, and insurers. They pay because FICO scores are embedded in regulatory and operational workflows as the default risk assessment metric. Consumers also pay via myFICO.com subscriptions.

Moat: Regulatory entrenchment + network effects + switching costs + brand. For decades, FICO was the only credit score accepted by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac for conforming mortgage origination — a major channel in the U.S. housing finance system. Source: FHFA. The "FICO Score" brand is embedded in consumer mindshare. However, as of July 2025, FHFA broke this exclusive mandate — see Section 4.

SECTION 4: MOAT ASSESSMENT

What protects this company from competition? - Regulatory entrenchment (now partially broken — see below) - Network effects: widely used by major U.S. lenders (exact adoption share: NOT FOUND in public data, but FICO states it is the industry standard used across the lending ecosystem) - Switching costs: entire lending workflows built around FICO - Brand: "FICO Score" is a household name

Moat trend: NARROWER. The evidence is concrete, not speculative:

Fact 1: On July 8, 2025, FHFA Director Bill Pulte directed Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac to accept VantageScore 4.0 as an alternative to Classic FICO for conforming mortgage origination — effective immediately. Source: FHFA.gov

Interpretation: This is the most significant regulatory change to FICO's business model in 30 years. The exclusive mandate that created the monopoly has been removed.

Fact 2: As of January 1, 2026, GSEs permit lenders to choose between Classic FICO and VantageScore 4.0 for each loan delivered. Source: FHFA FAQ

Interpretation: "Lender choice" means FICO must now compete on price and performance, not regulatory mandate.

Fact 3: A pricing war is underway. Equifax: VantageScore at $4.50/score through 2027 (free through 2026 for FICO customers). TransUnion: $4/score. Experian: free indefinitely, pledging 50%+ below FICO. FICO responded with Mortgage Direct at $4.95/score. Source: HousingWire

Interpretation: FICO's per-score pricing power in mortgage origination — previously unchallenged — is now under direct competitive pressure.

Fact 4: 42 billion VantageScore credit scores were used in 2024, a 55% increase from 26.9 billion in 2023. Source: VantageScore

Interpretation: General VantageScore adoption is accelerating, though conforming mortgage-specific share data is not yet available.

Important nuance: FICO is not passive. The Mortgage Direct License Program bypasses bureau middlemen. FICO Score 10T (trended data) is more predictive than Classic FICO. The Plaid/UltraFICO partnership adds real-time cash flow data. These moves could preserve dominance — but they also acknowledge the monopoly era is over.

Closest competitor: VantageScore 4.0 (jointly owned by Equifax, Experian, TransUnion). Gaining ground by regulatory mandate and aggressive pricing.

AI disruption test: LOW-MEDIUM. AI is not the primary threat — regulatory action is. AI could accelerate the shift via cash-flow-based underwriting that bypasses traditional scores for "thin-file" borrowers, but this is addressable-market erosion, not core-revenue displacement. FICO itself is incorporating AI (FICO Score 10T, Plaid partnership).

SECTION 6: THESIS & EXIT CRITERIA

Original thesis: FICO is the "toll booth" of the American credit system — a regulatory monopoly with near-zero marginal cost, extreme pricing power, and no viable alternative.

Nature of risk: STRUCTURAL. This is not a cyclical downturn or a bad quarter. The regulatory mandate that created the monopoly has been removed by FHFA policy. The competitive landscape has permanently changed. This distinction matters: cyclical risks resolve on their own; structural risks require the business to adapt or accept a lower earnings multiple.

Exit criteria (from our system): "VantageScore gains >30% GSE market share; CFPB regulatory action on pricing."

Has exit criteria been triggered or approached? - Fact: VantageScore is now approved for GSE use (Jul 2025) and implementation began Jan 1, 2026. - Fact: Conforming mortgage-specific VantageScore market share data is NOT YET AVAILABLE — implementation is only 2 months old. - Interpretation: The exit trigger has not been met numerically, but the preconditions are now in place. Direction of travel is clearly against FICO's monopoly. The question is speed of adoption, not direction.

Thesis status: WEAKENING.

The monopoly thesis is damaged. FICO is no longer the only option. But it remains the dominant option, and lending ecosystem inertia is enormous. Revenue is growing 16% and earnings are beating. The business is executing — it's the moat premium that's being repriced.

SECTION 7: VERDICT

Best-of-breed test: MAYBE. Historically an unambiguous YES — the monopoly scoring provider with no competitor. Today a MAYBE: still dominant, still high-margin, still exceptional FCF, but the regulatory monopoly is cracked and the trajectory favors competition.

Watchlist decision: KEEP (WATCH). Don't remove. FICO remains one of the highest-quality cash-flow businesses in the market. But reclassify from "premium compounder" to "moat-under-pressure compounder." The entry multiple must be LOWER now that competition is real.

Position decision: HOLD 1 share. DO NOT ADD at current prices.

We own 1 share at $1,623.97 (Jan 29 Track 2 tantrum buy, -21% as of $1,283).

Why not sell? - The position is ~$1,283 in a $40K+ portfolio — roughly 3% of capital. - The business is still executing (16% revenue growth, earnings beats, $739M FCF). This is not a thesis-broken NVO situation. - Selling to rebuy at $950 requires being right on both direction AND timing. VantageScore GSE adoption could stall (implementation is notoriously slow) and FICO could rebound before hitting $950. - Keeping 1 share forces us to monitor every earnings call and VantageScore adoption update — "skin in the game." - Acknowledged downside: We accept the risk that FICO can continue de-rating for years if the monopoly premium collapses further. At $1,283, a further 25% decline to $950 (our new START target) would add ~$333 to the unrealized loss on this 1 share. That's a tolerable cost for maintaining monitoring discipline on a $40K+ portfolio.

Why not add? - At $1,283 with a forward P/E of ~33.6x (on guidance), we are paying upper-mid peer range for a business whose moat is less secure than peers. - Insiders are selling aggressively while the moat comes under regulatory attack. - Our new fundamentals-derived START target is $950, which is 26% below current price. - Cash preservation for better entries is the system's core discipline.

SELF-ASSESSMENT: LESSONS FOR THE SYSTEM

1. We chased FICO. We bought 1 share at $1,624 on a Track 2 tantrum (Jan 29) — above our own $1,400 START target. The stock is now $1,283, a -21% loss. The VantageScore/FHFA threat was already public (announced Jul 2025). We mistakenly called this "multiple compression on a monopoly" when the monopoly itself was being dismantled.

2. The old targets were stale. $1,400/$1,300 priced FICO at ~37x forward earnings — a monopoly premium. With the monopoly cracked, those targets were too generous by 30–40%.

3. New system rule (proposed): Moat Deterioration Surcharge. When moat status changes from SAME → NARROWER during fundamental review, reduce START/ADD targets by 20–30% and require re-derivation from fundamentals (forward P/E × EPS guidance) rather than using historical price floors. This prevents anchoring on "where it used to trade" when the business reality has changed.

4. New template field (proposed): Nature of Risk label. Add STRUCTURAL / CYCLICAL / MIXED to Section 6 for every analysis. This prevents mixing "one bad quarter" with "the moat is changing."
